# Chapter 3 — The Hunter's House

Sound came first.

Voices. Muffled, like hearing a conversation through a thick door. They rose and fell in rhythms he couldn't follow. Angry. Pleading. Firm.

He tried to open his eyes. His lids were too heavy.

More words he couldn't catch. A name, maybe. Someone's name. Then footsteps. The creak of a door. Silence.

He slipped back under.

---

The next time he woke, there was light.

Pale grey through a shuttered window. A fire crackling nearby. The smell of something warm and savoury. His body felt heavy, his limbs buried under something rough and warm — wool. He was wrapped in wool.

He turned his head.

Low ceiling. Wooden beams. Walls of plastered wattle. A single room, small, with a hearth at one end and a bed of hay at the other.

A shape moved in his peripheral vision.

A girl. Young. Brown hair tied back. She was stirring a pot over the fire, humming something tuneless under her breath.

She turned. Saw him looking.

She smiled.

"You're awake!"

The words came at him fast. He caught the shape of them, the warmth behind them, but the specifics slid past him like water through a sieve.

She set down the ladle and walked over, wiping her hands on her apron. She crouched beside the hay bed and looked at him like he was a wounded animal that might startle.

She said something else. Longer this time. He caught *hungry* and *water* and maybe *hurt*.

He stared at her.

She waited.

He said nothing.

---

That first day was a blur of firelight and fragments.

She talked. He listened. She asked questions — he thought they were questions — and he responded with nods or shakes. She brought him broth and a chunk of dark, dense bread. She pointed at his bandaged knee and said something that sounded like a warning. *Don't move.* Or *Stay still.* Something like that.

He slept. Woke. Ate. Slept again.

The father appeared at some point. A big man with broad shoulders and a quiet face. He didn't say much. He looked at Eric for a long moment, nodded once, and sat down to eat.

Eric caught his name from the girl. *Kael.* Or maybe *Kaelen.* Something with a K.

The girl's name came easier. She said it herself, pointing at her chest, repeating it until he understood.

*'Lena.'*

---

The second day, he sat up.

His knee ached but the wound was clean, wrapped in fresh linen. His foot was the same. His palms were scabbed over. He felt weak and hollow, but the sharp edge of exhaustion had dulled.

Lena brought him a bowl of stew — thin, with chunks of root vegetables and shreds of something that might have been rabbit — and sat across from him, watching him eat.

She said something. He caught *cold* and *winter* and maybe *soon*. He nodded.

She sighed.

Then she started talking and didn't stop.

*"—and then the goat got into Old Marta's garden again, and you should have heard her. Screaming like the world was ending. Honestly, that goat's been a menace since spring and nobody does anything about it because it belongs to the elder's cousin, so of course it gets special treatment—"*

He caught fragments. *Goat. Garden. Screaming. Elder's cousin.* Enough to follow the shape of it.

*"—and the children have been impossible lately. Running through the square, kicking up mud, almost knocked over my washbasin yesterday. I told them if they did it again I'd tell their mothers, and they just laughed. Laughed! Can you believe it? Kids these days have no respect, I swear—"*

He ate his stew and nodded at the right moments.

*"—and the blacksmith's son. Don't even get me started. He looked at me today. You know the look. The one where they think they're being subtle but they're just being obvious and annoying. I'm going to give him a piece of my mind next time he tries it. See how he likes—"*

On and on. The neighbour's wash line. The well water tasting funny. the rabbit was tough today. Her father came home late and didn't say a word at dinner.

He didn't understand half of it. But her voice was steady and unhurried. She didn't seem to mind that he didn't answer. She filled the silence for both of them.

It was the most human sound he'd heard in fourteen years.

---

She ran out of things to say eventually.

The fire crackled. The shadows shifted on the walls. Eric set down his empty bowl.

Lena looked at him. Studied his face. Then she let out a breath and said something slowly, carefully, as if she knew he needed the words to come one at a time.

*"...sigh... who are you?"*

He understood that.

Every word.

He opened his mouth. The sounds sat in his throat, fourteen years old. He reached for them.

*"I am..."*

His voice was rough. Broken. He cleared his throat and tried again.

*"Eric."*

Lena's eyes widened. Her mouth fell open.

She stared at him for a long moment.

Then she lit up.

"You can talk!"

She said it fast, and he barely caught it, but her face told him everything. She leaned forward and the words came pouring out.

Where are you from? What happened? Do you remember anything? How long were you in the forest? Were you attacked? By what? Do you have family? How did you end up naked? Are you a criminal? Should she be worried?

He caught a third of it. Maybe less. He shook his head slowly.

*"I don't..."*

He paused. Searched for the word.

*"...remember. Much."*

Lena studied him. Then she nodded, as if that made perfect sense, and launched into another ramble about how the village elder once hit his head and forgot three days of his life.

Eric sat back and let her words wash over him. He understood more now. Piece by piece, the language was coming back.

---

By the fourth day, he was moving.

Not far. Just around the house. Testing his leg. Finding his balance. He carried water from the well, limping across the small yard with a bucket in each hand. The air was cold and bit at his cheeks. His breath misted. Winter was close.

He split kindling outside the door. The axe was light and the handle was worn smooth. He worked until his palms stung and his knee ached.

Kael watched him from the doorway once. Said nothing. Just watched.

Then he grunted and walked away.

Eric took that as approval.

The fifth day, he stepped outside the yard for the first time.

The village was small. He'd known that from his glimpse at the treeline, but seeing it up close was different. A single dirt path ran through the centre, packed hard by feet and cart wheels. Houses huddled on either side, their thatched roofs low and uneven. The palisade wall wrapped around everything like a protective arm, and the watchtower rose near the gate, its bell silent for now.

People looked at him.

An old woman stopped mid-stride, a basket of roots tucked under her arm. Two men by the well paused their conversation. A child pointed until his mother pulled his hand down.

Eric kept his head down and walked back inside.

He wasn't ready.

Later that day, there was a knock.

Lena opened the door. A man stood outside — middle-aged, lean, with a scar across his chin. He glanced past Lena and looked at Eric with narrow eyes.

He said something Eric didn't catch. Lena answered sharply. The man replied, his tone harder.

Eric caught one word.

*'Stranger.'*

Lena's voice rose. She pointed at Eric, then at the floor, then at the man. Her meaning was clear without translation: *He stays. This is my father's house. Not your business.*

The man left.

Lena shut the door and muttered something under her breath. Then she turned to Eric and smiled like nothing had happened.

She said something reassuring. He caught *don't* and *worry*.

He nodded.

But the knot in his stomach tightened. Not everyone wanted him here.

---

That evening, Lena served stew. Thick and hot. She portioned it out carefully — a full bowl for Kael, a full bowl for Eric, a smaller one for herself.

Eric noticed.

He looked at her bowl. Then at his own.

He pushed half of his portion into hers without a word.

She started to protest. Kael silenced her with a look. The meal continued in quiet.

Every day he understood more.

Not because anyone was teaching him. The words just started fitting together the more he heard them. Lena would say something and he'd catch a new piece, then another, and the gaps in his understanding filled in slowly, like water seeping into dry earth.

*Firewood.* The word Lena used when she pointed at the pile by the hearth. *Market.* The place she went every third day with a basket. *Elder.* The old man who made decisions for the village.

He stored them all. Built a mental map of sounds and meanings.

He also noticed how the village changed as the days passed. More smoke from more chimneys as families repaired their roofs and stacked firewood against their walls. Men hauling salted fish and dried meat from a smokehouse near the palisade. The scarred man from the well was always busy, always moving, always glancing at Eric when he thought Eric wasn't looking.

Winter was coming. The whole village was getting ready.

And Eric was sitting in a house that hadn't planned for a third person.

---

The sixth day, Kael came back with a rabbit.

He skinned it outside, quick and practiced, and tossed the pelt to Eric without a word. Eric caught it. Stared at it. Then he found a stretch of wall near the fire and pegged it up to dry. He didn't know if that was what Kael wanted, but it felt like the right thing to do.

Kael didn't say anything. But the next morning, there was a second pelt on the ground beside Eric's bed of hay.

Lena caught him looking at it and smiled.

*"He likes you,"* she said. Or something close. Eric caught *he* and *you* and the shape of a positive meaning.

He wasn't sure she was right. But it was something.

---

That night, he lay awake after the fire had died to embers.

The house was quiet. Kael's breathing was deep and even from his corner. Lena had curled up near the hearth, a thin blanket pulled to her chin.

Eric stared at the dark ceiling and thought about people he hadn't seen in fourteen years.

His mother. Her voice, calling him in before dark. That was all he had left of her. A single memory that played over and over.

His father's hand. Gripping his arm. The last touch he ever felt from his own world.

And his siblings. Benson and Elysia. He'd been six when he vanished. They would have been — he did the math slowly — Benson would be in his mid-twenties now. Elysia around eighteen.

Did they think he was dead?

Did they still look for him?

He pushed the thoughts away. They were too big. Too heavy. He couldn't carry them right now.

The embers crackled. A log shifted in the hearth.

He closed his eyes.

---

The next evening, Eric sat by the fire and stared at the flames. His hands were raw from splitting wood. His knee throbbed. The rabbit pelt hung drying on the wall.

He thought about the world he'd left behind. The convenience store. The park. The streetlights. It felt distant already, like a memory of someone else's life.

He thought about this world. The cold. The mud. The goat that got into Old Marta's garden. The scarred man who didn't want him here. The girl who wouldn't stop talking. The father who couldn't say what he meant.

He didn't understand this place. He didn't know its rules, its dangers, its unspoken lines. But he was learning. Piece by piece. Word by word.

The guilt was there, too. Sitting in his chest like a stone.

He was eating their food. Warming by their fire. Taking up space in a house that barely had room for two. Lena's portions were already smaller than his. Winter was coming. The scarred man was right about one thing — Eric was an extra mouth, and food was going to run short.

*'I need to earn my keep. I need to learn faster. I need to figure out where I fit.'*

But for the first time since he opened his eyes in that forest, he had a name. He had people who knew it. He had a wall with a drying pelt and a bowl of stew and a fire that hadn't gone out.

That would have to be enough for now.

He leaned back against the wall, let the heat wash over his face, and listened to Lena hum while she cleaned the pot.

The sound was still strange. But it wasn't unwelcome.
