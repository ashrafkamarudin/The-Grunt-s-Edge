# Chapter 5 — The Column

I follow the rider through the gate and I don't look back. The rider's already on his horse, and two others sit on the ground nearby with bundles beside them and clothes that don't fit — one about my age, square-jawed and trying to look bigger than he is, the other younger and narrower, keeping one hand on his pocket like he's checking something's still there. The rider looks at me and jerks his chin toward them. *"You're the third. Move."* He nudges his horse forward and the two others stand and follow, so I follow too.

---

We walk. The rider leads while the three of us fall in behind him, the younger one ahead of me and the square-jawed one dropping back after a while to look at me. *"You from Eastwood?"* I nod. He introduces himself as Darrin and jerks his head at the younger one — Tomas — who doesn't react, just keeps walking with his hand on his pocket. I ask why two from Oakfell and Darrin explains that the bigger village has a bigger quota, so they had to send two — him and Tomas, who hasn't said a word since they left. *"His father was angry,"* Darrin adds. *"Mother cried at the gate."* I don't know what to say to that, and neither does he, so we walk in silence for a while.

The road passes through farmland and forest and farmland again, the rider stopping once to let his horse drink without offering us any food, so we eat from our own bundles — hard bread and dried meat and whatever the villages gave us. Darrin talks sometimes about his village and the well everyone shares and his father who works the mill, and I nod at the right moments without offering much. Tomas eats alone, checks his pocket, and doesn't speak.

---

We reach the gathering point near evening of the second day — a crossroads with a well and a supply cart and riders standing around ignoring us, and then I see the crowd. Dozens of them sitting on the ground and leaning against trees and standing in small clusters with the same bundles and clothes that don't fit and the same empty look, at least sixty of them, and I keep my face still or try to while Darrin stops beside me staring. *"Wow... that's a lot more than I expected."* I nod but don't trust my voice yet, and even Tomas has stopped a few steps back, not checking his pocket anymore, just staring.

Our rider swings off his horse and walks toward a scarred man with a board and charcoal, and after exchanging a few words, our rider gestures toward us. *"Three. One from Eastwood. Two from Oakfell."* The scarred man makes marks on his board, glancing at each of us before making another mark. *"Eastwood. First time sending anyone in years."* *"First time for everything."* Then he walks away, leaving me standing there as more conscripts arrive and more riders report before I finally sit down against a tree and watch the gathering grow.

That evening, the mounted knight gathers us in the clearing near the well and waits until the talking stops, taking his time and letting the silence settle. The riders move through the crowd handing out spears — old shafts, some warped, some with heads that wobble, but spears nonetheless. *"You're marching to Grayford tomorrow at first light. That's your training ground. The march takes five days. Keep pace, follow orders, stay with the column."* He looks across the crowd. *"You're part of the military now. That means no trouble — not with each other, not with the riders, not with anyone we pass on the road."* He mentions that the road is relatively safe but stray monsters can wander through, unlikely but it happens, and if something attacks we use our spears — no formation, just stab it and try not to hit each other. He lets that sit while murmurs ripple through the crowd and people grip their new spears tighter, then adds that it's probably not going to happen anyway. *"If there is trouble I will deal with it."* He doesn't say how and doesn't need to. *"Now rest. We move at dawn."*

I look at the spear in my hands — old shaft, wobbling metal head, but sharp enough to cut. *'Monsters. I'm supposed to stab monsters with this.'* I almost laugh, almost, before the thought settles: *'...yeah. This is my life now.'*

---

The column forms the next morning at first light — about sixty of us stretched across the road with a handful of riders and two supply carts and the knight at the front, the rest of us holding spears we don't know how to use and wearing boots that don't fit. The knight calls something and the column lurches forward, and we march.

The first day is slow. The column stretches and bunches as the young ones fall behind and the older ones push ahead while the riders herd us back together, the knight never looking back. A rider mutters *"village stock"* in a tone I understand without knowing the words. I stumble twice in the first few hours, my feet catching on nothing, and the second time I nearly go down — someone behind me grunts but no one helps me up, so I get up on my own. *'This is harder than I thought.'* I don't know what I expected, but it wasn't this.

By midday my legs are screaming and my heels are raw where the boots rub, every step sending a hot spike up through my ankles. I'm thirsty, my throat dry from the morning dust, but I used the last of my water before noon. I see a man peel his boot off during a break and his sock is dark — couldn't tell if it was sweat or blood — and he puts it back on without saying anything. I look at my own feet and don't want to take the boots off, afraid of what I'll see, so I keep them on. A man asks a rider for water and gets told to shut his mouth and keep walking. I'm thirsty too, my tongue thick, and I try not to think about it but fail.

During the next break I sit down and finally pull off my right boot to find the sock damp and the skin underneath red and raw, with a blister the size of a coin already starting to swell. *'...great. Just great.'* I put the sock back on and the boot back on and don't take the left one off because I don't want to know. By evening the fifteen-year-old boy from the first day is limping, jaw tight, refusing to fall behind while the riders don't slow for him, and when I ask if he's alright during a break he just nods without meeting my eyes. I don't ask again.

At another rest stop, an older conscript sits next to me — grey in his beard, a scar across his knuckles, hands callused from years of work. He looks at my boots and asks if they fit, and I shake my head. *"Thought so."* He nods at the spear. *"You know how to use that?"* I tell him no. He grunts. *"Neither do I. Fifty years old and they hand me a stick with a nail on top."* He looks at his own spear. *"I've swung a hammer. Dug ditches. Carried sacks. Never stabbed anything in my life."* Then he looks at me. *"You?"* *"...Same."* He stares at the spear for a moment, then stands. *"Well. We'll learn, I suppose."* He walks back to the column and I sit with his words for a while. I don't see him again.

Darrin walks somewhere ahead and Tomas walks somewhere behind and I'm in the middle, the road keeping going.

---

The second day, people start talking — and then the real talk starts, the talk about where we're going and why. I catch it in fragments, walking near enough to hear without looking like I'm listening. *"—heard the fort's almost lost. They're pulling every warm body they can get."* *"That's just something people say. Forts don't get lost."* *"This one might. Something came out of the forest. Something big."* I drift past them to another cluster. *"My cousin's friend served up north. Said the monsters been acting strange for months. Aggressive. Moving where they don't usually go."* *"Like something's driving them."* The speaker notices me drifting close and stops talking, so I keep walking.

*'Monsters acting strange. A fort that might fall.'* I turn the fragments over in my mind without knowing what's true and what's just talk, but the fact that they're scraping villages for men like me — farmers and boys and old men with grey beards — tells me they're desperate enough to take anyone. *'Can I even learn to fight in time?'* I don't have an answer.

---

On the third day, two men fight — starts over nothing, someone stepping on someone's heel, someone pushing back, and suddenly there's shoving and fists while the column stops and people turn to watch. The riders don't shout; they just ride in, pull them apart, and march them to the front where they walk ahead of the knight's horse for the rest of the day, visible to everyone. No one fights after that.

On the fourth day, a man collapses — goes down mid-stride with no sound, just folded, and while the column stops, the knight doesn't. He keeps riding, calling back without turning: *"Get him up or leave him."* A rider gets him up and forces water down his throat, and the man stumbles forward while the column moves again. No one asks if he's alright.

That night, the rain comes — steady and cold and relentless, no shelter or dry ground or firewood that will catch, so we sit in the wet and wait for morning. I don't sleep, just lean against a tree shivering so hard my teeth ache while my feet burn from blisters I didn't know I had, too tired to take my boots off. Around me shapes huddle in the dark while someone coughs and someone else cries, trying to hide it, and my stomach is empty because the last of my bread was soggy from the rain. *'I want to go home.'* The thought comes out of nowhere, but I don't even know where home is anymore. *'This is only the march. Training hasn't even started yet.'* *'...I don't know if I can do this.'* *'Can't quit. There's nowhere to quit to.'* The knight doesn't give the order to stop; he just stops when the light is gone and starts again before it returns.

---

Before dawn on the fifth day, someone tries to run — I hear it more than I see it, shouts and feet on wet ground and then nothing. In the morning the man is back and the knight makes him stand in front of us. *"Next time I won't bring you back."* That's all — no shouting, no violence, just the fact. The man rejoins the column and we march.

*'He tried to run. They caught him. That's what happens.'* I think about it for a second, just a second — what it would be like to slip away at night and find a road heading south and disappear into a village where no one knows my name. *'...and do what? Go where?'* I don't have an answer. *'Doesn't matter. Can't run anyway.'*

I look at the road ahead and try to find something to feel — fear, anger, regret — but nothing comes yet, just the ache in my legs and the fire in my feet and the hollow in my stomach. I'm about to become a soldier and I don't know how to fight or if I'll survive training or anything at all. *'...please let the training at least be easier than the march.'* I almost snort at that, as if anything here is designed to be easier. *'...one step at a time.'* The road keeps going. So do I.

Grayford appears by midday — walls and stone and soldiers at the gate, and a sound ripples through the column, murmurs and exhales and someone saying *"finally"* under their breath, the word spreading like a sigh. Somehow we made it. I keep my face still, but inside something loosens — just a little — and then I step through the gate.
