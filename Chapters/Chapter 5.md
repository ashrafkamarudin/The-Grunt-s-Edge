# Chapter 5 — The List: Part 1

The morning was ordinary.

Eric woke to the sound of Lena complaining about the fire.

*"—died down again. I swear, if I have to rekindle this one more time before winter even starts, I'm going to scream."*

He sat up. The air was cold. His breath misted. He pulled the blanket tighter and watched her poke at the hearth with unnecessary force.

She noticed him watching.

*"Oh. You're awake. Good. Here, eat."*

She pushed a bowl into his hands. Root stew, thin but warm. He ate.

She talked.

*"The well's been tasting strange. I think something died in it. Old Marta says it's just the season changing, but Old Marta says everything is the season changing. Also, did you see the smoke from the scout tower this morning? No? Probably nothing. They've been jumpy lately."*

He caught most of it now. His understanding had grown faster in the past few days. Words that had been slippery were starting to stick.

*"Jumpy?"* he asked.

Lena shrugged. *"Soldiers passing the road more often. Not here, just... near. Further south. Someone said they saw a patrol last week."*

She said it casually. The way someone mentioned the weather.

Eric ate his stew and said nothing.

---

Kael was already outside when Eric finished eating.

He was standing by the woodpile, not doing anything. Just standing. Looking at the treeline.

Eric walked over.

*"Something wrong?"*

Kael didn't answer for a long moment. Then he said, *"Wind's shifting."*

That was all.

Eric stood beside him and looked at the trees. They looked the same as yesterday.

But he trusted Kael's silence more than most people's words.

---

The horn came mid-morning.

One long blast from the watchtower. Then another.

Lena dropped the ladle. Kael was already walking before the second note faded.

Eric followed.

The square was filling. Not running — people just stopped what they were doing and moved. The blacksmith wiping his hands on his apron. The old widow from the roof repair. A cluster of children who didn't understand but followed their parents anyway.

A rider stood at the center, beside the elder. Same tired horse. Same dark uniform. Same sealed scroll.

The elder raised his hand and the murmuring stopped.

He read aloud.

*"By order of the Royal Army. Each settlement is required to provide one able-bodied recruit for service. Alternatively, the settlement may pay an equivalent levy in resources. If neither is provided, the envoy will select by lot."*

Pause.

*"Deadline is dawn of the third day."*

Silence.

Then the murmuring started. Low at first, then building.

*"A levy? Now? Before winter?"*

*"They haven't done this in years. Not since—"*

*"Something must be happening. Bigger than we know."*

*"Monsters? The passes? I heard the northern garrisons are pulling back."*

*"Who told you that?"*

*"No one. But why else would they need more men?"*

The elder raised his hand. *"Enough. We don't know. We won't know. That's not our concern."*

A farmer called out, *"Not our concern until they take our sons."*

More murmuring. Someone else: *"Maybe it's just the regular rotation. Maybe they're short and this is normal."*

*"This isn't normal. A levy before winter isn't normal."*

The scout spat to the side. *"Normal doesn't matter. The order's here. We deal with it."*

Eric stood at the edge of the crowd.

One recruit. Or pay. Or random.

He looked at the village. The patched roofs. The thin smoke from chimneys. The faces of people who had barely enough for themselves.

They didn't have the resources to pay.

He knew it before anyone said it.

The elder raised his hand again.

*"We meet tomorrow morning. The household heads. We decide."*

The rider mounted his horse and rode out.

The village dispersed slowly, in twos and threes, talking in low voices.

Eric walked back to Kael's house.

Lena was already there, staring at the fire.

She didn't say a word.

---

The meeting was held in the communal storehouse.

A long wooden building near the square, used for grain storage and emergency gatherings. Benches had been dragged in. A brazier burned in the center, casting orange light across tired faces.

Eric sat in the back. He hadn't been invited — not explicitly. But Kael had looked at him that morning and said, *"Come."* So he came.

The elder stood at the front. Aldric was near him, arms crossed. A few farmers. The scout. Old Marta. Kael sat near the middle, silent.

The elder spoke.

*"You all know why we're here."*

He let the words sit.

*"One recruit. Two days. We don't have the coin or the goods to pay the levy. So we send someone."*

The scout muttered, low enough that everyone heard: *"They haven't called a levy in seven years. Not since the border wars."*

An old farmer leaned forward. *"Border wars were five years, not seven. The year the pass fell."*

*"Six,"* someone corrected. *"Six years. I remember because my cousin was in the second wave."*

*"Doesn't matter how many years. Point is they don't call a levy unless they need warm bodies."*

Aldric's voice cut through. *"We can sit here counting years all morning. Won't change the order."*

*"I'm just saying. Something's happening out there. Something we haven't heard about yet."*

The scout nodded slowly. *"More monster sightings this season than the last three combined. Traders are staying off the northern roads. Even the Hegemony patrols have been quiet."*

The elder silenced them with a look. *"We don't know what's happening beyond this valley. We may never know. What we know is the order. One man. Two days. Let's not waste time chasing rumors we can't confirm."*

No one argued.

But the words hung in the air.

*'Something's changed.'*

No one spoke.

The elder waited.

Still nothing.

*"Someone must say something."*

A farmer coughed. *"Well, it's not going to be my boy. He's fifteen, still learning the fields. Take him and the harvest dies."*

Another voice: *"My son has a bad leg. Has since birth. They won't take him."*

*"The blacksmith's apprentice is twelve."*

*"The miller is too old."*

A younger man spoke up. *"What about Silas? He's eighteen. He does nothing but drink and start fights. He's useless to the village."*

Murmurs. A few nods.

Then an older woman snapped: *"His mother will scream until the roof falls. You want to deal with that?"*

*"I'd rather deal with her screaming than send my son."*

*"She'll curse your name to the elders."*

*"Let her. The boy does nothing. He's a drain on every family here."*

The argument spiraled. Voices overlapping. One man defending Silas's family. Two others cutting him off. The elder had to raise his hand twice before the room settled.

Eric watched it unfold. He watched them tear at each other over a name that belonged here. Over someone whose mother would scream, whose family would feud, whose loss would leave a wound that festered.

Then he looked at himself.

No mother to scream. No family to feud. No history to protect.

The comparison wrote itself.

Excuses. One after another. Each person deflecting before the question could land on them.

Eric sat in the back and watched.

No one had mentioned him yet.

But he noticed the glances. Quick. Averted. The way people looked at someone when they were thinking about them but didn't want to be caught doing it.

The elder raised a hand. *"Enough. We know who can't go. Now tell me who can."*

Silence.

Then Aldric spoke.

*"We have someone."*

He didn't look at Eric. No one did. But the air changed.

The farmer from before: *"We don't even know where he came from. He showed up naked in the forest. No name. No family. No story. And now we're supposed to send him to the army? What if he's a criminal? What if he's running from something?"*

The scout: *"Does it matter? The army will figure him out."*

*"It matters to me. I want to know who I'm sending in my son's place."*

Eric sat still. Every eye in the room was on him now.

The elder said, *"Boy. Do you have anything to say?"*

Eric held his gaze.

*"I'm not a criminal."*

*"Then what are you?"*

He opened his mouth. The truth sat on his tongue — a different world, a different life, a light that swallowed everything. He could see their faces waiting. He could feel how absurd the truth would sound.

*"I don't remember."*

The farmer snorted.

The elder raised a hand. *"Lena told us the same. He woke up in the forest with nothing. No memory before that."*

*"Convenient,"* the farmer muttered.

*"Maybe."* The elder shrugged. *"But it's not the first time someone's wandered out of those trees with their head half-gone. The forest takes things. Sometimes it gives them back. Not always in one piece."*

He looked at Eric. His voice softened.

*"I've seen what he does around here. Quiet. Steady. Doesn't ask for thanks."*

He shook his head.

*"I don't know where he came from. But I know what kind of person he is."*

A pause.

*"That counts for something."*

Then Aldric's voice, flat and final.

*"He still has to go. None of that changes the quota."*

The farmer nodded slowly. *"We have to send someone anyway. Better someone we don't know."*

No one argued.

The farmer sat back. Unsatisfied. But silent.

Eric sat still. He hadn't known Lena had spoken for him. She'd done it without telling him. And some of them didn't believe it — he could see it in the farmer's eyes, in the way the old woman at the back was watching him. They didn't trust his story.

But they trusted their own logic more.

An old woman near the back said sharply, *"You mean the boy. The one who's been here two weeks."*

*"Yes."*

*"That's your solution? A boy who doesn't even know where he is?"*

*"He knows enough."*

*"He's a child."*

*"He's a man. And he's not ours to lose."*

The old woman opened her mouth, closed it. She didn't have a better answer. Only the refusal to accept the one in front of her.

*"He doesn't belong to a harvest. Or a hunting rotation. Or a family line."*

The farmer from before: *"He's strong. Works well."*

The scout: *"He's not tied to winter stores. Sending him doesn't reduce what we have."*

Another voice, lower: *"No family here. No one depends on him."*

Pause.

*"He's already eating our food."*

Each sentence landed like a nail.

Then the old widow stood up.

Marta. The one whose roof he'd fixed. She leaned on her cane and pointed a gnarled finger at Aldric.

*"You talk about him like he's a stranger. But he climbed onto my roof and fixed the thatch before the frost could get in. No one else did that this year. Not you, Aldric. Not anyone."*

She looked around the room.

*"He's a good boy. That matters."*

The old man from the fence repair grunted from his corner.

*"He helped me with the fence last week. Didn't ask for payment. Didn't ask for anything. Just saw an old man struggling and walked over."*

He looked at Eric.

*"That's rare."*

Aldric's jaw tightened. *"No one said he's a bad person. But good doesn't fill a quota. Good doesn't feed a village through winter."*

Marta sat down. She didn't agree. But she had no argument against winter.

Eric sat very still.

They weren't saying he was worthless. They were saying he was *loose*. A thread that could be pulled without unraveling anything. A variable that could be removed without changing the equation.

He understood the logic.

He just didn't expect it to sting this much.

---

*"Shut your mouths."*

The room went quiet.

Lena stood in the doorway. She must have pushed past someone to get there. Women didn't attend these meetings. But no one stopped her. Out of respect for Kael. A hunter's word carried weight, and his household had some leeway.

She walked forward until she stood in the middle of the room.

*"He's been here. He fixed Marta's roof. He split more wood than anyone in this room. He helped me carry water every morning without being asked. He fixed our shelf. Our shelf. My father had been meaning to do that for months. Eric did it in twenty minutes."*

She pointed at the farmer.

*"Your son is fifteen. You want to keep him safe. I understand. But Eric is not even—"*

She stopped. Her throat closed. She pressed her lips together, forced herself to continue.

*"—he's barely older than your boy. And you're sitting here talking about him like he's a sack of grain you can spare."*

The farmer looked down.

She turned to the scout.

*"And you. You said he has no family."*

Her voice wavered. She steadied it.

*"He has us. He's been sleeping in our house. Eating at our table. He fixed our shelf and carried our water and sat through my rambling every single night without complaining once. That makes him family."*

She stopped. Swallowed. Started again.

*"That makes him my brother."*

She said it like it cost her something.

The room went quiet.

The elder looked at Kael.

Kael sat motionless. His face was unreadable. He had said nothing all morning.

The elder said, *"Kael. You took him in. What do you say?"*

The room waited.

Kael looked at Eric.

Then he said, *"Ask him."*

Two words.

That was all.

---

The room turned.

Eric felt the weight of their attention like a physical thing.

They weren't debating anymore. They were waiting.

He looked at Kael. The man's face gave nothing.

He looked at Lena. She was already shaking her head.

He looked at the elder, at Aldric, at the farmers who had eliminated themselves one by one.

And he realized: they had reached this conclusion before he walked in. The meeting wasn't to decide *if*. It was to confirm *who*.

He should have felt angry.

He didn't.

He felt... logical.

Outsider. No blood ties. Physically capable. Already dependent on their resources. No political protection. No family to object.

The only option that didn't break the village.

*'This is insane. You're volunteering to go to war and you don't even know what the war is.'*

The thought came from somewhere deeper. A voice that hadn't spoken in days. The modern part of him. The part that had never thrown a real punch, never held a weapon, never been in a fight that wasn't a schoolyard scuffle.

He'd watched war movies. Played video games. Read books about soldiers.

That wasn't the same. That wasn't even close.

*'You're going to die.'*

He pushed the thought down.

*'Maybe. But if I stay, someone else dies. Someone who belongs here.'*

He stood up.

*"I... I will go."*

The stutter surprised him. He hadn't planned it. His throat had tightened around the words and forced them out in two pieces instead of one.

He cleared his throat.

*"I will go."*

Lena's voice broke the silence.

*"No."*

Eric looked at her.

*"No. You don't have to. They can't make you. You're not even from here."*

*"That's why,"* he said.

She stared at him.

*"That's exactly why."*

---

The meeting ended without ceremony.

People filed out in silence. A few glanced at Eric as they passed. Not hostility. Not gratitude. Something in between.

Aldric was the last to leave. He stopped at the door. Looked back at Eric.

*"You didn't have to..."*

He didn't finish the sentence. Just shook his head and walked out.

Eric stood alone in the storehouse. The brazier crackled.

He tried to feel something.

Relief. Fear. Regret.

A small voice in the back of his head whispered: *You just volunteered to die.*

He pushed it down.

*'It was the logical choice.'*

*'That doesn't mean you have to be okay with it.'*

*'I am okay with it.'*

*'No. No, I am okay with it.'*

Both voices were his. They sat in his chest at the same time, pulling in opposite directions, neither willing to let go.

*'This is the right thing to do. Yes. This is.'*

He pressed his palms against his eyes until he saw colors.

*'I am okay with it. I have to be okay with it.'*

He said it again. Slower. Firmer.

*'I am okay with this.'*

When he lowered his hands, the room was the same. The benches. The brazier. The door.

Nothing had changed.

But he had said yes.

And he couldn't take it back.

He stood there for a long time, staring at the empty benches, the dying fire, the door that everyone had walked through on their way out.

It didn't feel like satisfaction.

It felt like the end of something.
