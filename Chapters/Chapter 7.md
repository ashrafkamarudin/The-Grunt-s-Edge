# Chapter 7 — Shield Line

The morning starts before the sun.

Right after waking up, we're marched to the training yard and stopped in front of a stack of shields, wooden and tall, piled on the ground like leftover scrap. No one hands them out and no one tells us to take one — we just stand there until someone at the front realizes what's expected and bends down to grab one, and then the rest of us follow.

I pick mine up by the grip and my arm drops before I'm ready, the weight pulling my shoulder down hard until I adjust and lift again, doable but barely. The shield is heavier than it looks — tall enough to reach past my ribs, with a slight curve to the front and iron banding along the top and bottom edges. The wood is thick enough that I can't feel my fingers through it. It's not a soldier's shield. It's a wall on a handle.

Around me men struggle with theirs — one nearly tips over from the weight, another drags his across the ground because he can't get a proper hold, a third tilts his at the wrong angle. No one corrects them. Not yet.

After we've all picked up our shields, a soldier walks down the line and drops a short sword at each of our feet — no sheath, no instruction, just the blade on the ground in front of us — and we bend down to pick it up ourselves. I hold the sword in one hand and the shield in the other, knowing nothing about either of them.

The instructor walks to the stack of shields without saying a word. He picks one up, plants his feet, and raises it chest-high — the edge at eye level, his body solid behind it. He holds it there for a few seconds, then drops it. The clatter echoes across the yard.

He walks back to the front and turns to face us. His voice carries across the whole yard.

*"Raise your shields! Shoulder to shoulder!"*

He gestures at the space between us.

*"That is your line! Hold it for dear life! Don't break formation!"*

He waits.

*"This is all you will learn for these two weeks. So focus."*

---

The first hour is a disaster.

The instructor puts us shoulder to shoulder with shields overlapping, front facing forward, but no one knows how to stand or where to look or what to do with their feet. The man beside me holds his shield at the wrong angle, and I don't know enough to tell him. The man past him drops his before the drill even starts, the clatter loud in the morning air.

The instructor doesn't shout. He walks down the line grabbing shields and forcing them into position, shoving a man's shoulder back and kicking at someone's foot until it moves where he wants it. He doesn't explain why.

*"Hold it there."*

Then they hit us — a group of men pushing against our shields — and we fold immediately, the line crumpling like paper with men stumbling backward and shields tilting and gaps opening everywhere. Someone falls. Someone else drops his sword. I'm pushed into the man beside me and we end up in a heap while the instructor watches without a word.

*"Again."*

We untangle and reset and try again. It goes the same.

*"Again."*

The third time, I brace harder and manage to stay upright, but the man to my left goes down and takes two others with him. The instructor walks over and stands above them.

*"Get up."*

They get up.

*"Again."*

It takes six tries before the line holds for three seconds.

When it finally breaks again, I'm bent over with my hands on my knees, gasping, my arms shaking from the weight and my shoulders burning and my legs threatening to fold. The man beside me looks the same. We don't look at each other. We just breathe and wait for the next push.

---

The next two days blur into repetition — drill, reset, drill, reset, the sun moving across the yard as we move with it. By day three my body has stopped screaming, not because it doesn't hurt anymore but because screaming takes energy I don't have; my shoulders are a steady burn that starts at dawn and doesn't fade until sleep, my legs have stopped shaking during holds, and my grip on the shield has found a resting position that doesn't cramp my fingers. We still fail — not as often as day one, but often enough — and the instructor never explains why or tells us what happens if we break, just watching and correcting the shape of the line in silence.

The food comes at noon, the same every day, and by day three I finish my bowl still hungry — not starving, just not enough — and I scrape the sides and look at the empty bowl feeling something I don't want to name. Across the table, others scrape their bowls too, but no one says anything.

---

Day four. The soreness never left. I wake up tired with my arms heavy before I lift the shield, and I know I'm not alone — I see it in the men around me, slower movements and quieter mornings and fewer words before formation. Midway through the drill, a man near the front of the line drops his shield. Not in protest or rebellion — he just stops holding it, his arms giving out until the shield falls forward and the line bends around him.

The instructor notices. He walks over and stands in front of the man. The man tries to pick the shield up. It slips. He tries again. It slips again. He looks up at the instructor, waiting.

The instructor doesn't react. He just turns and walks away.

Two soldiers pull the man out of formation. The line reforms with the gap filled by the man behind him. No one talks about it. The drill continues.

That evening, I see movement near the food cart. A man — larger than most of us — reaches across and takes a piece of bread from a smaller conscript's portion. He doesn't hide it. He does it openly, like it's already his. The smaller man looks at the bread, then at the larger one, and does nothing. He doesn't ask for help. He doesn't even look up.

I watch it happen. My mouth opens slightly before I catch myself — almost speaking without deciding to. The larger man hasn't noticed me yet. There's a split second where I could still look away, pretend I didn't see.

I don't look away. Not because I'm brave. Because I can't.

Then the larger man catches my eye.

*"...What? You want to help him? Stay in your place."*

I glance around the room. No one else is reacting. Not a single person. They're all eating, staring at their bowls, pretending they didn't see anything.

I look down at my own bowl. Pause. Pick up my bread. 

*'I should have said something. But I didn't have it in me. Not tonight.'*

I feel something — a flicker of guilt, the urge to say something — but my body doesn't move. I'm too tired. Too hollow. I don't have the strength to stand up for someone else when I can barely stand for myself.

I eat what's mine without looking up again.

Across the room, someone stands up. I don't look at first — I don't want to see the bully again — but the movement draws my eye anyway. It's not the bully. It's Darrin.

He walks past my spot without looking at me. He stops at the smaller man's table and sets down a piece of bread from his own portion — not dramatic, just a quiet act. Then he walks back to his seat without saying a word.

The bully watches him go. Says nothing.

Darrin sits down like nothing happened.

I stare at my empty bowl. I don't know what to make of it.

---

Later, lying in the dark, I listen to the barracks settle around me. Men lie awake — I can tell by the rhythm of their breathing. Someone nearby is massaging his hands slowly, methodically, like he's trying to convince them to work again tomorrow. Someone else is breathing too hard to sleep, trying to slow it down, failing.

A few bunks away, someone is crying. Quiet. Trying to hide it. The younger one — the boy from the march, the one who was limping. He doesn't make any noise. Just the shape of it in the dark.

I stare at the ceiling. Don't say anything.

My mind drifts — not to the drills or the bread or the crying boy, but somewhere else. A convenience store on a Friday night. A park bench overlooking dark water. A life that felt empty, but at least it didn't hurt like this.

That life is gone. And now I'm marching toward worse.

I stare at the ceiling until my eyes adjust to the dark.

---

Sleep comes eventually. Then morning. Then another morning. They start to blur the same way the drills do.

My body adapts — I'm stronger, I can hold the shield longer without my arm shaking — but underneath it the fatigue is permanent, never fully resetting, so I go to sleep tired and wake up tired and the tiredness becomes the baseline I operate from. The instructor demonstrates a stabbing motion once — a short push forward with no wind-up, the blade finding an invisible gap. Then we do it over and over. Not enough to master it. But enough to know which way the blade points when the shield comes down.

The men around me have changed too, quieter and more mechanical, not talking during meals anymore. I see Darrin once during a formation break — further down the line, shield lowered, face blank with exhaustion. He looks like everyone else. Our eyes meet. He nods. I nod back. That's it.

I start noticing the structure underneath the drills. He prioritizes the line above everything else; when someone collapses, he corrects the gap instead of the person because the individual doesn't matter but the shape does. The edges suffer most and the center survives most, the line rotating implicitly — not taught, just absorbed through repetition. I don't fully understand what I'm seeing, but I sense it.

---

Day ten. A fight breaks out during evening meal — not over anything important, just a space on the bench or a look that lasted too long, and suddenly two men are on the ground with fists swinging while the room goes quiet. A soldier walks over and grabs one by the collar, pulling him off and shoving him toward the door while the other follows without being told, and the fight ends as fast as it started. No one really reacts — a few men look up, then back at their bowls. I realize that a few days ago I would have watched longer. Now I just eat.

In the morning, one of the bunks near the back is empty. The man who slept there isn't at formation. No one asks where he went.

The next day, the instructor sends a heavier push than usual during drill. I feel it before I see it — the vibration through the shield, the collective weight pressing against us. The shield edge bites into my palm hard enough to leave a mark. My grip locks. My breathing breaks apart, trying to find a rhythm, failing, gasping through the strain.

The man beside me starts to fail first. I see his arm drop incrementally, his shield tilting forward, the gap between us threatening to open. I can't help him. I can't hold him and hold myself. I just keep my own shield up and wait for the gap to happen.

It doesn't.

Someone behind him fills the space before I can see it happen. The line bends but doesn't break. We hold.

When the push stops, I'm gasping. The release is almost worse than the hold — my body doesn't know what to do with itself. No one collapses. No one falls out.

The instructor doesn't praise us. He just nods once and walks away.

That evening I sit on my bunk and look at my hands. The blisters from the march have turned to calluses, my grip stronger, my arms looking different — not bigger, just harder. I realize I've stopped thinking about my shoulders.

*'We're not learning. We're being shaped.'*

I don't know if there's a difference.

---

Day twelve. The announcement comes while we're standing in the yard after the final drill. A soldier I haven't seen before walks to the center and reads from a piece of paper.

*"Training ends today. You are needed at the frontline. Report to your barracks. Pack your equipment. Be ready to move at first light."*

For a long moment, no one moves. The words hang in the air, not landing, like they need time to travel from the ears to the brain. A beat of silence where no one processes it — not because they didn't hear, but because hearing doesn't mean understanding.

Then someone laughs. A short, broken bark from somewhere in the crowd — the wrong reaction, cut off as quickly as it started. Someone drops their gear — metal clattering on stone, the sound of shock moving through someone's hands before their mind caught up.

I look for the instructor. He's already gone. Didn't stay to watch us react. Didn't care.

Then the murmurs start.

*"Two weeks. They said two weeks."*

*"We're not ready."*

*"Ready or not, we're going."*

A voice louder than the rest — somewhere behind me, rough and tired. *"This is a joke. We can barely hold a line. They're sending us to die."*

Heads turn. No one answers him. The soldier who read the announcement is already gone.

More voices rise, overlapping. Someone argues that they should refuse. Someone else says refusing gets you shot or worse. A third voice cuts through — flat, resigned: *"Doesn't matter what we want. They'll march us out either way."*

The argument dies as quickly as it started.

I stand there looking at the training yard, the packed dirt and the worn posts and the dummies at one end, the same as it was on day one. The only thing different is us.

*'We didn't finish. We got reassigned mid-process.'*

*'...and we didn't even get a day to rest.'*

I walk back to the barracks after that.

---

Packing takes no time — I own nothing except what I'm carrying, shield and sword and token and clothes — so I tie the bundle and sit on my bunk while other men do the same around me. No one talks or celebrates or argues because there's nothing to argue about; the decision was made somewhere above us and we're just the bodies that move.

We march out before dawn through the dark streets of Grayford until the gates open and we step through them — the same gate we entered ten days ago. The soldier from the latrines never reappeared. I didn't expect him to. I look back once, but the training yard is somewhere behind those walls and I can't see it anymore, so I turn forward and keep walking.
