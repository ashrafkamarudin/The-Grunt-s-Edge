# Chapter 6 — The Road

The next morning came too fast.

Eric opened his eyes and stared at the ceiling. The fire was already lit. Kael was already gone. Lena was moving around the house, quiet, not looking at him.

He sat up.

The bundle was on the floor beside his bed of hay. Kael must have left it. Eric picked it up. Heavy. Dried meat, hard bread, a small pouch of something he didn't check.

He dressed. Stood. Waited.

*"Lena."*

She stopped. Didn't turn.

*"Thank you. For what you said. At the meeting."*

She stood there for a long moment. Her shoulders dropped.

*"I didn't say it to be thanked."*

*"I know."*

She turned. Her eyes were wet, but she wasn't crying.

*"You're an idiot."*

*"I know."*

She shook her head. Then she walked over to a small chest near her bed and pulled something out.

A knife. Worn handle, short blade, leather sheath.

She held it out.

*"It was my brother's."*

Eric looked at it.

*"I can't—"*

*"Take it."*

He took it.

Lena didn't say another word. She went back to her chores.

He stood there, holding the knife, and felt the weight of it. Not the blade. The meaning.

He tucked it into his belt and walked outside.

---

Dawn was breaking. Pale grey light, thin frost on the ground.

Kael was standing by the woodpile — the same spot he'd been standing yesterday, the day before, every morning Eric had known him.

He wasn't looking at the treeline this time.

He was holding the bundle.

*"Food. For the road."*

Eric took it.

*"The road south. Two days to the garrison town. Tell them you're the recruit."*

Eric nodded.

Kael looked at him. His jaw worked.

*"When I hunt,"* he said slowly, *"I don't chase what runs. I wait where it will pass."*

He held Eric's eyes.

*"Move early. Stay quiet. Watch the ground before the sky."*

A pause.

*"Same applies anywhere."*

Then, so quiet Eric almost missed it:

*"Come back."*

Eric didn't know how to answer that. He didn't know if it was possible. He didn't know if he'd survive a week in this world's army, let alone return to a village that had just sent him away.

*"I'll try."*

Kael nodded once. Then he turned and walked inside.

Eric stood there. Then he adjusted the bundle, touched the knife at his belt, and walked.

---

The rider was waiting at the gate.

Same dark uniform. Same tired horse, rested now. He was leaning against the gatepost, chewing on a piece of dried meat. He looked at Eric, looked at the bundle, looked at Eric again.

*"You're the one?"*

Eric nodded.

The rider grunted. He spit out the last bit of meat and swung onto his horse.

*"Two days. Try to keep up."*

He nudged his horse forward and passed through the gate.

Eric followed.

---

The village passed in silence.

He passed the well. The bucket was still there. He passed the storehouse. The door was closed. He passed Marta's house — the roof he'd fixed was holding against the frost.

At the gate, he stopped.

He turned and looked back.

The village was small. Patched roofs. Dirt path. A handful of lives clinging to the edge of a forest.

Somewhere inside, Lena was awake. She'd reach for the water bucket and remember she'd have to carry it herself.

Kael was standing by the woodpile. Looking at the treeline.

The shelf was still holding.

He touched the knife one last time.

Then he turned forward and followed the rider.

The road stretched south, pale in the early light. Bare trees lined both sides. Frost clung to the grass. The wind was cold and carried the smell of distant smoke.

The rider didn't look back.

Eric didn't either.

---

The first mile was numb.

His feet moved. The road passed beneath them. The rider stayed ahead, a dark shape on a dark horse, never close enough to speak to, never far enough to lose.

Eric's mind was blank. Not calm. Just empty. The way a room is empty after someone clears everything out of it.

The second mile, the thoughts started.

*I'm doing this. I'm actually doing this.*

He looked at the rider's back. The man hadn't said a word since the gate. He didn't seem interested in conversation. He seemed interested in reaching the garrison town and being done with this errand.

*What kind of person rides around collecting conscripts for a living?*

The rider shifted in his saddle. Spat to the side. Kept going.

*Someone who's done it before.*

The third mile, the fear settled in properly.

It didn't come as a wave. It came as a slow drip, filling a tank he didn't know was there. The road was unfamiliar. The trees were unfamiliar. Every sound — a bird, a branch, the wind — was a sound he couldn't identify.

He was in a world he barely understood, walking toward a future he couldn't predict.

And he was alone.

The rider didn't count. The rider was a function. A delivery system.

Eric was the package.

---

They stopped at midday.

The rider dismounted beside a stream, let his horse drink, and sat on a rock. He pulled out more dried meat and bit off a chunk.

He didn't offer any to Eric.

Eric sat on the ground, a few meters away. He opened the bundle Kael had given him. Hard bread. A strip of dried meat. A small handful of dried berries.

He ate slowly. Watched the water move over the stones.

The rider said, *"What's your name?"*

*"Eric."*

The rider chewed. Swallowed.

*"You from around here?"*

Eric considered the question.

*"No."*

The rider grunted. Didn't ask where.

---

They walked through the afternoon.

The road curved through sparse forest, then open fields, then forest again. The wind picked up. Eric's legs ached. His boots — Kael's old boots, too big, stuffed with rags — rubbed against his heels.

He didn't complain.

He didn't know if complaining was allowed.

By evening, he couldn't feel his toes.

The rider stopped at a clearing. A shallow cave in the rock face, big enough for two people and a horse.

*"We stop here. Fire's your job."*

Eric looked at him.

The rider raised an eyebrow.

*"What? Never built a fire?"*

Eric had. In a different world. With a lighter and firestarters from a camping store.

*"I'll figure it out."*

The rider snorted and walked away to tend to his horse.

Eric gathered dry wood. Used the knife — Lena's knife — to shave bark into fine curls. Struck two stones together until a spark caught.

It took forty-five minutes.

The rider didn't help.

But when the fire caught, he nodded once and sat down beside it.

*"Not bad."*

Eric said nothing.

But he felt the small warmth of it, and not just from the flames.

---

They ate in silence.

The fire crackled. The horse shifted. The wind moved through the trees.

The rider spoke eventually.

*"First time?"*

Eric didn't pretend to misunderstand.

*"Yes."*

The rider nodded. Stared at the flames.

*"You'll survive or you won't. Same as everyone."*

He said it flatly. Not cruel. Not kind. Just factual.

Eric lay back, his head on the bundle, and stared at the cave ceiling.

*You'll survive or you won't.*

He'd known that before he left the village.

But hearing it out loud made it real.

He listened to the rider's breathing even out. To the horse shifting its weight. To the wind moving through the trees beyond the cave mouth.

He thought about Lena. Wondered if she'd eaten. Wondered if she'd already stopped reaching for the water bucket before remembering.

He thought about Kael. Standing by the woodpile. Looking at the treeline.

He thought about the shelf. Still holding.

*I'm not going to see that shelf again.*

The thought arrived without warning. Clean and sharp.

*I'm not going to see any of it again.*

He closed his eyes.

*You'll survive or you won't.*

He didn't sleep for a long time.

---

The second day was harder.

His heels were blistered. His legs were stiff. Every step sent a dull throb up through his knees. The road seemed longer than it had the day before, and the rider seemed further ahead.

He caught himself thinking about the modern world more than he had in days. Not the big things — not the park, not the convenience store. Small things. The way a bus seat felt after a long shift. The sound of a refrigerator humming. The clean taste of tap water.

He'd taken all of it for granted.

He'd never get any of it back.

*Stop. You're on a road. Focus on the road.*

They stopped once for water. The rider let him drink first this time. Didn't say anything. Just pointed at the stream and waited.

Then kept going.

The forest thinned. The ground flattened. The air changed — less woodsmoke, more dust. The trees gave way to open fields, brown and harvested, with scattered farmhouses in the distance.

Near midday, the rider pointed ahead.

*"There."*

Eric looked.

A wall. Stone. Higher than the village palisade. A gate, iron-bound. A tower with a flag he didn't recognize.

The garrison town.

They walked the last mile in silence.

Eric's heart was beating too fast.

*You'll survive or you won't.*

The gate loomed closer.

*Same as everyone.*
