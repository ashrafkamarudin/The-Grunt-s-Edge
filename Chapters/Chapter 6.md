# Chapter 6 — Grayford

The column stops just outside the gate without anyone telling us why. The knight captain rides ahead alone toward the wall while we stand in the road, not moving or speaking, five minutes or maybe ten passing before the gate opens. We shuffle forward and at the entrance we're told to leave our spears, stacking them against the wall, and I lay mine down with the rest without looking back.

I step through the gate and into Grayford.

*'First city I've seen in this world. And I'm here as a conscript, not a visitor.'*

The thought passes quickly. I don't have time to sit with it.

---

The first thing I notice is the noise — orders being called and equipment rattling and boots on stone, the sound of a place that runs on movement and function, with wide purposeful streets lined with stone buildings built for use instead of comfort. Soldiers move through them in ones and twos while a patrol passes without looking at us. I stop walking for a second, just one. *'...looks like medieval Europe.'* The words come out without thinking and no one hears them, and I don't even know why I said them before a rider calls something and the column moves again and I follow.

---

We're led to an open square with a table set up near the center where a clerk sits behind a ledger, a charcoal board beside him with numbers and tallies I can't read. One by one we're called forward — name, age, origin, a wooden token handed over, step aside, next — and when my turn comes I step up to the table where the clerk doesn't look at me. He asks for my name and age and origin, and when I say Eastwood he glances up for the first time. *"Eastwood. First time sending anyone, isn't it?"* I don't know, so I say so. He asks if I've ever handled a spear or any weapon before, and I tell him no. He asks what work I did. *"...Odd jobs. Labor."* He reaches into a box and pulls out a wooden token, reads it quickly, and slides it across the table before calling the next person.

I pick it up — a string of characters carved into pale wood, GRF and some numbers I don't understand. *'GRF742. Something fifty-one. This is who I am now.'* I tuck it into my pocket and wait.

Then I'm waved toward a table where a man in a blood-stained apron grabs my foot without asking and pulls off my boot. He looks at the heel, at the raw blister, at the red skin around it, then dips a cloth into something that smells sharp and wipes it across the wound while I hiss through my teeth. *"Don't be soft."* He smears a paste over it that stings, wraps it in clean cloth quick and tight, and lets go before moving to the other foot — worse, the blister already burst, pink and wet underneath. He does the same thing faster this time. *"Keep them dry. Change the wrap if it gets wet. You'll be fine."* He's already reaching for the next person before I've finished putting my boot back on. I stand and walk back to the others, my feet feeling better — not good, but better.

*'...at least they patched me up. Means they want me alive. For now.'*

---

After the roll call we're sorted. A man walks through the crowd without asking questions, just looking and pointing — the bandaged hand to the left, the broad-shouldered conscript who's swung an axe before to the right, and me to the middle. I watch the left group get led away toward the supply yard and the right group get taken somewhere else while the middle stays where we are. No one explains what any of it means, but I understand enough.

We're marched to a barracks block — Barracks C, Lower tier, West hall — and the smell hits me before I see anything, sweat and damp wool and unwashed bodies and hay that hasn't been changed in weeks. Rows of bunks stacked three tiers high with narrow gaps between them, men already lying in some of them watching us enter with the tired eyes of people who've been here before us. I'm assigned to Row three, bunks twelve to twenty, and I find mine on the top tier — a thin lumpy straw mattress and a folded cloth for a pillow, the space barely wider than my shoulders. I sit down or try to, my hands fumbling with the laces on my right boot, swollen from the march, and I have to try twice before the knot gives. The wrapping underneath is clean but I can already see a faint stain, the blister still throbbing. I look at it, then look away and put the boot back on without bothering with the left.

Around me the hall hums with noise — conversations, someone coughing, someone arguing about a bunk, a laugh somewhere in the back. Across the aisle a man tries to wrap his own hand with a strip of cloth torn from his shirt, working one-handed and struggling with the knot while no one helps him. Further down someone sits on his bunk staring at the wall without moving or speaking. Everyone here has something — a limp, a bandage, a silence that says more than words — and the smell is everywhere.

---

A sergeant appears at the door without shouting, and the noise dies on its own as he walks through the center of the hall speaking as he goes. *"You form up at first light. If you're late, you run until I decide you've run enough. You don't leave this barracks without permission. If you wander, you'll be found."* He pauses. *"You eat when food is served. Miss it, you wait for the next meal. Your sleeping space stays clean. If it attracts rats, you sleep outside with them. Fighting among yourselves earns punishment duty. Drawing blood earns the whip. You speak only when spoken to by an instructor or an officer."* He stops near the far wall and turns. *"You don't know anyone here. You don't owe anyone anything. Keep your head down and do your work. Lose issued equipment and you replace it. Can't replace it, you work it off. Break formation without orders and you answer for it."* A final pause. *"Run and you'll be brought back. If you're not brought back, no one will come looking."* He looks across the room once, then walks out.

Silence holds for a few seconds before the noise returns, quieter than before. I lie back on my bunk staring at the underside of the wooden frame above me while my feet throb and the smell doesn't go away and sixty men settle into an unfamiliar dark around me.

*'...tomorrow it starts.'*

I don't know if I'll sleep. I don't know if I want to.

---

I don't remember falling asleep, but when the door slams open and metal hits the first bedpost I'm awake. Two soldiers move through the barracks banging on bunks with sticks and shouting — *"Up! Up! Move your feet!"* — and the noise is everywhere, metal on wood and boots on stone and voices overlapping, and I'm off my bunk before I know I'm moving, my right foot hitting the ground and the blister sending a sharp reminder that yesterday wasn't a dream. *"Did I say stretch? I said up! Get your boots on, get outside, move!"* Around me men scramble, someone tripping over his own boots and going down hard, another on the top bunk landing wrong and cursing, another still tangled in his blanket while a soldier's stick slams against the post beside his head. A conscript near the door fumbles with his belt and gets grabbed by the shoulder and shoved through the doorway. *"Outside! Now! Don't make me tell you again!"*

We push toward the door without order or line, just bodies pressing forward, someone shoving past me and me stumbling into the man ahead of him. Outside the air is cold and the sky is still dark as conscripts spill into the open space near the barracks — men still pulling on their shirts, a few barefoot, someone missing a boot — and we stand there in the cold, not quite ready, waiting for whatever comes next.

---

An officer stands at the front, waiting until the shuffling stops before he speaks — not loud, just flat, tired before the day has started. *"Levies."* He says it like it tastes bad. *"I'm supposed to train you in two weeks. That's what I was told."* He shakes his head slowly. *"I've seen bad batches before, but you're something else."*

A mutter from somewhere in the crowd, low but not low enough. The officer's head turns. *"Someone has something to say?"* Silence. Then a man steps forward from the middle — broad-shouldered, older than most of us, the same one who sat next to me during the march and asked about my boots. He doesn't shout. *"We been walking for days. We're hurt. We're hungry. And you talk to us like we're dirt."* He pauses. *"We're not dogs."*

The officer watches him without interrupting, then nods slowly. *"No. You're right. You're not dogs."* He gestures and two soldiers move in. The man shoves the first one off — strong — but the second one hits him behind the knee and he goes down, held there face in the dirt. The officer walks over and looks down at him. *"Dogs are useful."* He turns and walks back while the man is pulled to his feet and marched away, nose bleeding, holding his ribs, not looking back.

*'...don't be that guy.'*

I look at the officer. I look at the spot where the man was standing. Then I don't think about it anymore.

*"I don't want to be here. You don't want to be here. But here we are. So here's how this works — you follow orders, you don't fight each other, you don't talk back. Do that and I won't have to notice you."* He pauses. *"You get one day because you're too broken to train. After that, you move when I say, eat when I say, sleep when I say. Two weeks. Then you're on your way to the north-east fort. I'll teach you what I can in that time. The rest you'll learn by staying alive — or you won't."* He points at two men near the back, the ones from the fight on the march. *"You two. Faster next time."* He doesn't explain why he chose them and walks away.

---

A soldier leads us through Grayford as the sun rises, pointing and walking without looking back. *"Latrines. Use them before formation, not during."* I break off toward the latrines, passing a soldier sitting near the wall who watches me go. When I come back he's still there, resting against the stone like he never fully decided to stand up again, and his eyes track me for a second longer than necessary. *"You're from one of the Eastwood batches?"* Not a statement — a guess. I pause. *"...Yeah."* He gives a small grunt like that confirms something unpleasant. *"Thought so. Same route I came in on, years back. Eastwood line, Oakfell line... all the same mess. They don't change much."* I wait, but he doesn't look like he's in a teaching mood — more like he's remembering something he didn't want to. *"Listen."* Not commanding, just tired warning. *"Don't assume what happens here is training."* I frown. *"Then what is it?"* A short laugh with no humor. *"Sorting. They see who breaks, who adapts, who dies quietly enough not to slow the rest down."* He tilts his head toward the barracks. *"Stick close to whoever looks like they've been through it before. Don't try to be clever. Don't try to stand out."* He pauses. *"And don't expect them to notice if you die. They don't care."* He waves lightly. *"Go on."*

I turn and walk back, the exchange staying with me longer than it should. *'Sorting. Not training. I don't know which is worse.'* I file it away.

*"Mess hall. You eat when food's served. If you're late, you don't eat."*

We file into a long building with benches and tables where we're given bowls of porridge — thick and warm — with dark bread and dried meat. I eat slower than I expected to, and around me I hear low murmurs. A conscript across the table looks at his portion. *"Not bad. Thought they'd give us scraps."* Someone else grunts in agreement. Another one — younger, the one who was limping during the march — almost smiles. *"Maybe it won't be so bad here."* No one argues. It's not much — I'm not full when I finish — but it's more than I thought they'd give us.

Then a few tables over, a man stands up and pushes his bowl off the table, muttering something I don't catch. A soldier behind him says something low, but the man doesn't sit down. Two soldiers lift him by the arms and drag him out, his heels scraping across the stone floor while the bowl stays on the table untouched. *'...don't know what he did. Don't need to know. Just need to not do it myself.'*

*"...don't stop. Keep moving."*

The soldier at the door gestures and we follow. Bathhouse — schedule simple, evening after training, no exceptions. *"This way."* Infirmary — I recognize the man in the blood-stained apron, but he doesn't look at me. Training yard — open ground, packed dirt, posts and lines and dummies at one end, where we'll spend the next two weeks. *"Keep moving."* Parade ground — where we form up, where the officer stands. By the time we've walked through every location the sun is dropping, my feet aching better than yesterday but the wrapping shifted and the blister rubbing with every step, not healed but healed enough.

We're led back to the barracks and no one speaks.

---

I sit on my bunk while the barracks hums with sixty tired men around me, someone already asleep and someone else talking in a low voice and the smell still there. I take out the wooden token and look at it — the letters and numbers still meaning nothing to me, but meaning something to this place — and tuck it into my shirt.

*'Well... this is it. My military life begins.'*

*'...I wonder if anyone will remember my name.'*
