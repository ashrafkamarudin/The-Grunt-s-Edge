# Chapter 8 — The Aftermath

Before the march, we're stopped at a supply depot near Grayford's gate. Armor is handed out — leather and iron, basic and worn, no one checking fit or size. I strap mine on as best I can. It shifts when I move. The edges dig into places I didn't know I had.

We form up while a sergeant walks the line, stopping at each man. He checks the straps, glances at the shield, nods or grunts. No conversation — just verification.

When he reaches the front, he turns. *"Fort Lowen. Northeast frontier."* A pause. *"That's where you're going. When I say March, you move and you don't stop until we get there."*

He looks down the line.

*"March!"*

The gates open and we move. Riders flank the column on both sides, guiding us forward, pushing stragglers back into formation with short shouts so no one falls behind. No one really tries to.

The march is shorter than the last one, but way harder. The armor straps cut into my shoulders within the first mile. I adjust them once, twice, a third time... and each shift only delays the burn, never fixes it. The spear shaft knocks against my thigh with every step, a constant rhythm I can't sync with, and no matter how I shift my grip or angle the head, it just keeps finding my bone. The men around me are no better — pulling at straps, shifting weight, muttering — but no one ever fully fixes the problem. We just find a way to carry it on until the march ends.

And then there's the shield. The shield grows heavier with every mile — not because it's actually heavier, but because my arm is running out of strength to hold it. I shift it from one shoulder to the other, but the relief lasts minutes before the burn comes back. The edge digs into my collarbone. The strap cuts off circulation. I try carrying it by the grip instead, but the weight pulls my arm down and the edge knocks against my hip until I lift it back to my shoulder.

*'...I hate this shield.'*

The thought comes unbidden. I don't even try to finish it. I just carry the shield and hate it and carry it some more.

By the time the fort appears on the horizon, my body is complaining in new ways I don't have names for yet.

---

Fort Lowen comes into view slowly — walls of dark stone, a gate flanked by torches, banners I don't recognize hanging limp in the still air. There's smoke rising from somewhere inside, thick and grey, not like chimney smoke.

The column slows as we approach the gate. The march commander rides ahead, exchanges words with someone at the wall, then disappears inside. We wait. No one tells us why.

After a long moment, he returns. His face doesn't give anything away.

*"Battle's over. We just missed it. They're still clearing the field. We wait here until someone tells us otherwise."*

No one asks what it cost. No one needs to.

He gestures forward. The gates open.

We pass through.

The first thing I notice is the sound of someone screaming, a high broken noise somewhere to the left that cuts off and starts again. Then there are boots running past before I can see who, and someone shouting for a stretcher. The column presses forward as there's no room to stop.

*'What is this smell...'*

Then the smell hits. It reeks of blood and mud and something else I can't place and i almost puke back everything I ate that day. Then the bodies. Several rows of them laid out near the wall, covered with cloth that isn't quite long enough. Soldiers walk past them without looking. A man is being carried on a stretcher, his leg wrapped in red-stained linen, his eyes fixed on nothing. A medic crouches beside another man sitting against the wall, head in his hands, rocking slightly.

I see a dead soldier near the path. Not laid out with the others but just lying where he fell, waiting. His eyes are open, not staring at anything, just open. A small pouch is clutched in his hand. A personal object no one has pried loose yet.

My step falters. For a moment I can't move — chest tight, hands cold, the thought arriving before I can stop it: *'Am I going to end up like that?'* I look at the body and I see myself — same uniform, same dirt, same empty sky. I don't know if I can survive this. I don't get enough training to prepare me for this.

Then the voices start around me — the choked sounds, the mutters, someone trying to turn back — and I realize I'm not alone in being afraid. That doesn't make me brave. It just makes me one of many.

I keep walking.

Behind me, someone makes a choked noise, cut off before it becomes words. A mutter from ahead, quiet: *"Oh gods..."* A voice to my left, uncertain: *"Is that... is that one of ours?"* No one answers.

Another conscript stops walking. Someone further back tries to turn around — a rider cuts him off, shouting, forcing him back into formation. A voice rises over the crowd: *"I can't— I can't be here—"* cut off by the rider's shout. Someone else mutters, lower, not cruel just scared: *"Shut your mouth and walk."*

The column stutters but doesn't stop. The riders push us through.

I keep walking because there's no other choice.

---

We stand in the yard for what feels like a long time.

Injured soldiers are led past. Others sit against walls, being wrapped or left alone. Someone nearby is crying. Not loud, just a broken sound that won't stop. A medic crouches beside a man on the ground, repeating *"Stay with me"* in a flat voice like he's said it a hundred times today.

A few paces away, a clerk calls out numbers while another marks a board with charcoal. *"Fourteen dead. Twenty-three wounded. Three more from the east wall."* His voice is tired, routine. He could be reading a supply list.

A pair of soldiers walk past carrying a body between them. They lay it down near the row. One of them pulls a tag from the neck, glances at it, and calls the name to the clerk. Then they flip the body over — face-down — and walk away to get the next one.

The tag was the only time the man had a name after death. And it lasted about three seconds.

I file this away without deciding to.

---

A clerk sets up at a table near the yard. A ledger opens. A tired voice calls names.

One by one, we step forward. Show our token. State our name. Wait while the clerk finds the line and marks it.

When my turn comes, I hand over the token. He reads it, writes, and hands it back. *"Group seven. Tent row three."*

He's already calling the next name before I step away.

Darrin is after me. Same group. Same tent.

A third from our batch comes with us — a face I recognize from Grayford but never learned the name of. That's it. The three of us. The rest of the column goes elsewhere.

We're led through the fort. The walls rise higher than I expected — stone and timber, reinforced at the corners, with walkways running along the top where soldiers stand watching the treeline. Between the walls, the fort is a maze of paths and tents and makeshift structures. A blacksmith's hammer rings somewhere out of sight. Horses are being led past. A soldier carries a stack of spears across the yard.

I try to take it all in. There's too much to hold at once.

Darrin walks beside me. Neither of us has said anything since processing.

*"...well. We made it."*

His voice is flat. Not relief. Just acknowledgment.

*"Yeah,"* I say. *"We made it."*

We keep walking.

There are no barracks here. Not enough room. Instead there are tents — rows of them, canvas and rope, packed dirt floors, pegs hammered into the ground to hold them down.

We stop at one of them.

*"This is yours."*

---

Inside, the tent holds ten men. Most are already there when we arrive. They look up when we enter, then look away.

Someone is in the center. Seems like the leader. No one introduces him as the leader. He just picks his spot first, sits, drops his gear, and the others shift around him without being told. That's how you know.

I take a spot near the edge of the tent. Darrin settles beside me. The third from our batch — someone mutters his name as *Wells* — finds a corner.

A voice from inside — not Corbin, someone else. *"Oh.. New faces. So you're the newbies joining us?"* Not hostile. Just tired. *"Welcome. Sit wherever. It's your tent now too."*

A pause. Then someone asks, more casual than curious: *"So... what's your name?"*

Before anyone answers, a voice from the far side cuts in — flat, tired. *"Does it matter? Tomorrow a few of us are going to die. Names are wasted here."*

The tent doesn't react. No one argues.

After a beat, someone else speaks — not defending or contradicting, just explaining: *"Don't mind him. He's just... stressed. But he's not wrong. Few of us die every battle, so..."*

A shrug.

*"I'm Renn. You can remember it or not. Doesn't matter."*

He shifts, settling back against the canvas.

*"I'm kind of new here too. Got here a week before you lot."*

He gestures with his chin around the tent.

*"That's Sutter. He's been here the longest. Pell's the quiet one. Mila doesn't talk much."*

He points toward the front.

*"Corbin's the squad leader. Doesn't say much either. But he knows what he's doing. He's the strongest here."*

A pause.

*"They're not usually this grumpy. But we just finished a battle and..."* He lets the word hang. *"...we lost three of our own. You three are their replacements."*

He nods toward the tent entrance.

*"...Gren and Larris are still in the medical tent. Gren hurt his arm, and Larris his leg. Last I heard, the healers expected them back in a day or two."*

The tent goes quiet. Not hostile. Just heavy.

I look around properly for the first time. A man in the corner has his arm wrapped in linen, the edge of the bandage dark with dried blood. Another moves slowly, favoring his ribs. No one here came through clean.

Renn looks at us. Not unkindly.

*"So. Welcome to the squad."*

The tent goes still. Not the silence of agreement — the silence of no one wanting to answer. A beat that stretches longer than comfortable.

Wells hasn't spoken since we entered. He found a spot near the back, pressed against the canvas like he's trying to take up as little space as possible. His eyes move around the tent, measuring everyone, saying nothing.

No one argues. Corbin doesn't react. He just lies back, eyes on the canvas. The silence passes.

There are no bedrolls. No straw. Just the packed dirt and whatever we brought with us. I spread my gear on the ground and lie down. The earth is cold beneath me.

Someone mutters from across the tent: *"Mess is at the east end. If you miss it, you don't eat. No one's going to tell you when."*

I file that away.

---

That evening, the tent settles into darkness. No one sleeps. Not yet.

After a long silence, Renn's voice comes out — slower than before, like he's forcing himself to make conversation.

*"...So. Where you lot from."*

Not a question anymore. Just a statement with a pause at the end.

Darrin: *"Oakfell."*

A gap. Then I add: *"Eastwood."*

A longer gap. Mila's voice, delayed: *"...Eastwood? That's way up north, isn't it."*

*"...Yeah."*

Wells mutters something — his village, maybe. No one follows up.

Another silence. Then Renn: *"Fallmoor. Further out."* He says it like it doesn't matter.

The quiet stretches. No one fills it.

Then Darrin: *"...You just finished a battle."*

Not a question. He knows.

Renn doesn't answer right away. When he does, his voice is flat.

*"East treeline. They came out of nowhere. Wave after wave. You hold until you can't."*

He stops. Doesn't finish.

Mila's voice, a beat too late: *"Well~ I'm lucky to survive this time too."*

The sing-song falls flat. No one laughs. But Renn exhales — almost a snort.

Sutter, after a pause: *"Too stubborn to die."*

*"I told the monsters I had laundry to do."*

A short exhale from someone. Not quite a laugh. Close enough.

Darrin, quiet, not hesitant: *"Then we'll hold it better next time."*

Long silence. No one answers at first.

Then Renn, delayed: *"...Yeah."*

The conversation doesn't end. It just fades. Someone asks about the mess. Someone answers. A fragment about the cook. A complaint about guard duty. The words come slower. The gaps grow longer.

At some point, I realize Pell has stopped responding. His breathing changed — slow, even. Asleep.

No one comments.

Sutter, after a while: *"...We should sleep."*

No one argues.

Mila, quieter now, words slurring: *"Try not to die tomorrow."*

Pause.

Sutter: *"...You too."*

---

The tent goes quiet. The canvas shifts in the wind. A distant horn sounds somewhere beyond the walls — not an alarm, just a signal. Someone calls back in the dark.

I lie on the packed dirt and stare at the canvas above me. The conversation didn't end. It just ran out of words before it ran out of night.

I still don't sleep. But it's easier than it was an hour ago.
