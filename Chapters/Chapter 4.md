# Chapter 4 — The Weight of a Name

He woke before dawn.

The fire had burned low. The house was still. Kael's corner was empty — already gone. Eric lay on his bed of hay and stared at the ceiling for a long moment, then sat up.

His knee was stiff but the sharp pain had faded to a dull ache. He pressed his palm against the wound. Dry. Healing.

He got dressed in the clothes Lena had left for him two days ago — a rough wool tunic, a pair of patched trousers, a rope for a belt. They were too big in the shoulders and too short in the leg. They smelled like smoke and old sweat.

They were the best clothes he'd ever owned.

He found Kael outside, sharpening a knife by the door.

Eric stood there for a moment. Then he said it, slowly, forming each word with care.

*"Is there... anything... I can help?"*

Kael looked up. Studied him for a long moment.

Then he grunted and pointed at the woodpile.

Eric got to work.

---

The axe was dull and the wood was knotted. By mid-morning, his hands were blistered, his shoulders ached, and the pile had grown by half.

Lena came out with a cup of water. She watched him split a log, then handed him the cup.

"You talk more today."

He caught the words. Understood them. Took a drink before answering.

*"A little."*

She smiled. "Good. It was getting boring talking to myself."

He almost laughed.

---

That afternoon, Lena put him to work inside the house.

There was a shelf that had been sagging for months. She pointed at it. He looked at it. Then he found some nails, a flat stone to hammer with, and fixed it in twenty minutes.

Lena stared at the shelf like it was magic.

"You just... fixed it?"

He shrugged. *"I did... this kind of work. Before."*

He didn't elaborate. But for a moment, he thought about the renovation crew he'd worked for two summers ago. The foreman who'd taught him to measure twice and cut once. The way a well-built frame felt solid under his hands. Some things translated no matter what world you were in.

She shook her head. "My father's been meaning to do that since spring."

*"He's busy."*

"He's a man. He'd rather hunt all day than fix one shelf."

Eric didn't know how to respond to that, so he said nothing.

Lena laughed. "You're useful to have around."

He didn't know how to respond to that either.

---

The next day, he helped a widow repair her roof.

She'd spotted him walking past and called out. Her voice was old and cracked, but her eyes were sharp. She pointed at a loose patch of thatch and said, *"You. Young. Fix it."*

He climbed up. The thatch was old and brittle. He spent two hours weaving in fresh reeds, working slow, making sure it would hold.

When he came down, she handed him a piece of dried meat and said, *"You're not useless."*

He took that as a compliment.

---

That afternoon, he met more of the village.

Lena needed to fetch water from the well, and she dragged him along. The well was the heart of the village — a circle of packed earth where women gathered with buckets and men lingered between tasks. They all looked at Eric when he arrived.

An old woman with a sharp face said something to Lena. Eric caught *outsider* and *clothes* and maybe *still here?*

Lena answered without missing a beat. He caught *father's house* and *none of your business.*

The old woman sniffed and turned away.

A younger woman — maybe mid-twenties, with a baby strapped to her chest — gave Eric a small smile. He nodded back.

Lena filled her buckets and they walked home.

She muttered the whole way. He caught *nosy* and *old hag* and *every day the same.*

He said, *"She doesn't... like me."*

Lena snorted. *"She doesn't like anyone."*

He thought about that for a moment.

Then he said, *"Good."*

Lena laughed. A real laugh, surprised out of her.

*"You're strange,"* she said.

He didn't disagree.

---

The scarred man found him on the third afternoon.

Eric was splitting wood behind Kael's house. The rhythm was coming easier now. Swing, crack, split. Swing, crack, split. His blisters had burst and scabbed. His shoulders had stopped screaming.

He didn't hear the man approach until the shadow fell across him.

He turned.

The scarred man stood there, arms crossed, jaw tight. He was shorter than Eric but broader, built like a stump. His eyes were pale and hard.

He spoke slowly, as if talking to someone slow.

*"You. Outsider."*

Eric set the axe down. Waited.

*"How long?"*

Eric understood the question. *"A week. Maybe."*

The man's jaw tightened. *"You eat our food. You sleep in our houses. You do not belong here."*

Eric said nothing.

*"Winter is coming. You know what winter means? Food runs out. People starve. And you sit here, eating what should go to our children."*

Eric met his eyes. Kept his voice flat.

*"I work."*

*"Not enough."*

They stood there. The wind moved between them. Somewhere, a dog barked.

Then Lena's voice cut through.

*"Aldric!"*

She was standing at the corner of the house, hands on her hips, eyes blazing. She marched over and stood between Eric and the scarred man, pointing a finger at his chest.

He caught *my father's house* and *his business* and *get out before I—*

Aldric held up his hands. He said something Eric didn't catch, but his tone was mocking. He looked at Eric one last time, shook his head, and walked away.

Lena stood there, breathing hard, watching him go.

She turned to Eric.

*"Ignore him."*

He nodded.

But he didn't forget.

---

That evening, Lena was in a mood.

She'd been quiet since the Aldric incident, but quiet for Lena meant she was thinking. She stirred the pot with more force than necessary, tossed in a handful of wild herbs, tasted it, frowned, muttered something under her breath.

Eric sat by the fire, watching her.

He said, *"You are... angry."*

*"I'm not angry."*

The pot clanged against the hearth.

Eric waited.

She sighed. *"Fine. I'm angry. Aldric has no right to talk to you like that. You've done more for this village in a week than he's done all year."*

*"He's lost something."*

Lena stared at him.

*"I don't know what,"* he continued, slow. *"But I know... the way he looks at me. That's not hate. That's... hurt."*

Lena's shoulders dropped.

*"You're too forgiving."*

*"I'm not. I just..."* He searched for the words. *"I know what that look means."*

She didn't ask how he knew. He was glad she didn't.

She went back to stirring the pot, but the anger was gone.

---

Later, she taught him a word.

They were eating by the fire. Lena pointed at her bowl and said, *"Stew."*

He nodded. He knew that word.

She pointed at the fire. *"Fire."*

He nodded again.

She pointed at him. *"Eric."*

He blinked.

*"Eric,"* she repeated, slower. *"Your name. Eric. Did I say it right?"*

He opened his mouth. Closed it.

*"...Yes."*

She smiled. *"Good. Because I've been calling you 'strange silent boy' in my head and it's getting tiring."*

He snorted. The sound surprised him.

Lena's grin widened.

*"You laughed."*

*"I didn't."*

*"You did. I heard it."*

He looked away. But the corner of his mouth was still turned up.

---

That evening, Lena was quieter than usual.

She stirred the pot. Added a handful of dried herbs. Eric sat by the fire, watching the flames.

He said, *"Aldric. He is... always like that?"*

Lena shrugged. *"He lost his son last winter. Monster got him."*

Eric absorbed that.

*"So he hates strangers."*

*"He hates everyone. But strangers more."*

They sat in silence for a while.

Then Eric said, *"Kael. Why did he... take me in?"*

Lena's hand paused over the pot.

She didn't answer for a long time.

Then she said, quietly, *"I had an older brother."*

Eric waited.

*"He died. Ten years ago. He would have been your age."*

The fire crackled.

*"My father doesn't talk about it. But I know. When he saw you at the meeting, he said..."* She trailed off. Shook her head. *"He said you looked like someone who needed a chance."*

Eric stared at the flames.

Ten years ago. A brother who would have been his age. A father who couldn't say it out loud but who saw a stranger collapse at the treeline and recognized something.

He didn't know what to feel.

*"I'm sorry,"* he said.

Lena shook her head. *"It's not your fault."*

She stirred the pot.

*"It happens. Out here. People hunt, people get hurt, people die. My mother died bringing me into the world. My brother died in the forest. That's just... how it is."*

She said it flatly. Not bitter. Just factual.

*"You learn to keep going."*

She went back to stirring the pot.

The silence stretched, but it wasn't uncomfortable.

---

Two days later, Eric was helping a neighbor repair a fence.

He'd volunteered. Walked up to an old man who was struggling to lift a beam and just started helping. The old man had looked at him, grunted, and pointed at where he wanted the nail.

They worked in silence for an hour. When they finished, the old man said, *"You work hard. For a stranger."*

Eric nodded.

The old man said, *"Stay for dinner."*

It wasn't a question.

---

That night, Eric sat with the old man and his wife and ate a bowl of fish stew. The old man talked about his youth, about monster hunts and bad winters and the year the river froze solid. Eric caught maybe half of it. It was enough.

*"You ever fought?"* the old man asked suddenly.

Eric paused. *"...No."*

*"Thought so. You move like someone who hasn't been in a real fight. You're careful. Careful is good. Keeps you alive."*

The old man's wife sniffed. *"He's going to scare the boy."*

*"Boy needs to know. Winter's coming. And winter brings more than snow."*

Eric set down his spoon. *"What do you mean?"*

The old man leaned forward. *"Monsters get desperate when it gets cold. They come closer to villages. Happens every year. Some years worse than others."*

He tapped the table.

*"That's why we have the wall. That's why we have the watchtower. That's why every able hand needs to know which end of a spear points toward the enemy."*

Eric absorbed that.

*"You think I should learn?"*

The old man shrugged. *"I think a man who can split wood can learn to hold a spear. Rest is up to him."*

---

On the way back, he passed the well. A group of men were gathered there, talking in low voices. The lamplight cast long shadows across their faces.

He caught fragments. *The list... the call... quota...*

Then one of the men noticed him. The talking stopped.

Eric kept walking.

But the words followed him home. *The list. The call. Quota.* He didn't know what they meant. But the weight in their voices told him it wasn't good.

He thought about Kael, who had taken him in for reasons he couldn't say out loud. He thought about Lena, who gave him half her portion without being asked. He thought about the widow's roof and the old man's fence.

He thought about Aldric. Who was cruel, but who wasn't wrong.

*The list. The call. What does it mean?*

He had no answer.

---

The next morning, he heard the horn.

A long, low blast from the watchtower. Then another.

Lena's head snapped up. She dropped the bowl she was drying and ran to the door.

Eric followed.

A rider was coming through the gate. A man in a dark uniform, riding a horse that looked too tired to be carrying anyone. He reined in at the center of the village and dismounted stiffly.

The elder was already walking toward him.

Eric stood at the corner of Kael's house and watched.

The rider pulled a sealed roll of parchment from his saddlebag. Handed it to the elder. Words were exchanged — short, formal.

Then the elder read the parchment.

His face didn't change.

But his shoulders dropped.

He turned and looked at the villagers who had gathered. He said something. Eric didn't catch it all.

But he heard enough.

*Conscription... one man... before first snow.*

The rider mounted his horse and rode out.

The village was silent.

Eric stood there, the word turning over in his mind.

*So that's what the list was. That's what the call meant. Conscription.*

The pieces clicked into place.

He thought about Kael, who had taken him in for reasons he couldn't say out loud. He thought about Lena, who gave him half her portion without being asked. He thought about Aldric, who wasn't wrong about one thing — Eric was an extra mouth.

*If someone has to go...*

He didn't finish the thought. Not yet.

*Conscription.*
