# Chapter 2 — The Forest

He opened his eyes.

The sky was lighter. Not daylight, but a shift toward gray. The black bleeding into something softer. Dawn was still an hour off, maybe two. The trees were starting to take shape around him, their outlines no longer swallowed by pure dark.

He was still naked. Still on the forest floor. The ground was cold against his skin. The air bit at his exposed shoulders and chest.

His breath misted faintly in front of his face.

For a moment he just lay there, staring up at the pale gaps between the branches, and tried to remember how to be a person.

Then it hit him.

Not the cold nor the hunger. The *situation.*

His breath shortened. His chest tightened. The rational part of his brain, the part that had spent fourteen years learning to analyse and plan and stay calm, was drowned out by a surge of pure animal panic.

*'I'm in another world!'*

He sat up too fast. The world tilted. His vision swam.

*'Shit! I'm in another world and I don't know anything!'*

He scrambled to his feet. His legs were stiff and his muscles moved slow. He stumbled, caught himself on a tree trunk, and stood there breathing hard.

The forest stretched in every direction.

Endless. Dark. Unfamiliar.

*'whaa... haa... which way? where do I —'*

His heart was hammering. He could hear the blood in his ears. The river was somewhere to his left, he could still hear it, but the river led where? He didn't know. He didn't know anything.

He started moving.

Not in a direction. Just *moving*. His feet carried him through the underbrush, branches scraping his arms and chest, leaves slapping against his thighs. He tripped over a root and caught himself, kept going. His breath came in ragged gasps.

*'Stop. Think. Think...'*

But he couldn't. The panic had its claws in him. Six years old again, standing naked on a sidewalk in a world he didn't understand. Except this time he was twenty and the world was supposed to be *his*, but he didn't remember any of it.

He kept running. Frantically searching for something, anything. A landmark. A sign. A person. A road. A fucking bus stop. Anything that told him where he was or where to go.

There was nothing.

Just trees. Just more trees. Endless and identical and silent.

So he kept running.

Branches whipped his face. Thorns raked his arms. His feet — soft, modern feet that had never walked on anything worse than pavement — took the punishment of roots and stones and sharp debris. He didn't slow down.

He couldn't slow down.

The forest blurred past him. His lungs burned. His legs ached. The cold was still there, gnawing at his skin, but the heat of exertion drowned it out. He ran until his muscles screamed. He ran until his chest felt like it was splitting open.

He ran until he couldn't run anymore.

His pace dropped to a stumble. Then a walk. Then he was leaning against a tree trunk, chest heaving, spit stringing from his lips, vision spotting at the edges.

He stayed there for a long time. Bent over. Gasping. Shivering now that he'd stopped moving.

His head was pounding. His throat was raw. Every breath tasted like blood.

*'Tired.'*

The thought was simple. Childlike. There was no room left in his skull for complex panic. Just tired. And cold. The cold was still there.

*'The cold. Focus on the cold.'*

His skin was tight. His muscles were stiff. The shivering had settled into a steady tremor that wouldn't stop.

Naked on the forest floor, exposed to the wind, he could feel his body losing heat with every minute he stayed still.

*'I need to move. Find shelter ... or something.'*

No shelter. No dry leaves to burrow in. Nothing to start a fire with. Which meant the only option was to find people. Which meant the river.

He pushed off the tree. His legs were unsteady. Everything hurt.

He took a step. Then another. His foot came down on a root and he stumbled, caught himself, kept moving.

A branch caught his foot.

He went down hard.

His palms hit the ground first. Sharp gravel and dead twigs biting into his skin. His knee followed, slamming against a rock that sent a bolt of pain up his leg. He lay there for a moment, face inches from the forest floor, breathing in the smell of wet earth and rotting leaves.

The pain cut through the fog.

He pushed himself up. Looked at his hands. The palms were scraped raw, tiny beads of blood welling up where the skin had broken. His knee was worse. A deep gash just below the kneecap, welling red, mixed with dirt and small stones.

*'haa... I'm bleeding.'*

The thought was absurdly calm.

*'Great. Now I'm bloody too.'*

He sat there on the cold ground, naked and bleeding, and felt the panic drain out of him. Not because he was okay. Because his body had simply run out of adrenaline.

He took a breath.

Then another.

*'Okay.'*

He looked at the cut on his knee. The bleeding had slowed but the wound was dirty. Full of grit and small bits of leaf.

*'That's going to get infected. shit.'*

He stood up. His knee throbbed. He put weight on it and winced. It held.

The river was still audible, maybe thirty meters away. He limped toward it.

---

The bank was wide and rocky. The water moved fast, clear enough to see the dark shapes of stones beneath the surface.

*'Looks clean.'*

He stared at it.

*'Which means absolutely nothing.'*

He didn't know what was in that water. Mud. Dead things. Who knew what. One bad sip and he'd be shitting himself to death before he ever found help.

But dying of thirst would happen a lot faster.

*'Pick your poison.'*

He cupped his hands and drank. The water was cold and clean-tasting. He drank until his throat stopped aching, then sat back on his heels and waited.

Nothing happened. No immediate sickness. Good. That meant nothing.

*'Upstream or downstream.'*

He looked at the current. Too tired to think properly. His brain kept slipping off the question.

*'Downstream. Lowlands. More people. Probably.'*

He didn't have a better reason.

He stood up. His knee was bleeding properly now, blood running in a thin line down his shin. The cut on his foot stung. The cold was still there, gnawing at his skin. He was already tired and he hadn't walked anywhere yet.

He started walking.

The river led him through the forest. He kept to the bank where the ground was firmer, avoiding the underbrush that scraped at his bare legs. The wind cut across the open water and bit into his wet skin. His teeth chattered. His arms were wrapped around his chest, but it didn't help much.

After about an hour, the forest began to change.

The trees thinned. The undergrowth became less wild. He started noticing signs. A broken branch that looked deliberately snapped. A patch of ground where the leaves had been trampled flat. The remains of a fire pit near the bank.

His chest tightened again, but this time it wasn't panic.

It was hope.

He followed the signs for a while then suddenly a path emerged. Narrow, barely visible, but a path nonetheless. Worn dirt between the trees. Footprints pressed into the mud.

He followed it.

The sun was fully up now, pale light filtering through the canopy. The air was marginally warmer, but the wind hadn't let up. His shivering had become constant, his limbs heavy and slow. The cut on his knee had scabbed over with dried blood, but each step pulled at it and fresh red seeped through the crack. Nothing dramatic. Just a slow leak he couldn't afford.

His head was starting to feel light. The edges of his vision flickered when he blinked.

*'Just a little further.'*

*'ha... just a little further Eric.'*

*'You can do it... you need to.'*

The path curved around a thicket of bushes, and then he saw it.

Smoke.

Thin columns rising above the trees. Chimney smoke. People cooking breakfast.

He quickened his pace. His knee screamed. His scraped hands throbbed. He didn't care.

The trees opened.

A village sat in the valley below.

Small. Maybe fifteen buildings huddled together behind a wooden palisade. Sharpened logs standing eight feet tall, with outward-angled stakes hammered into the ground at the base to stop anything from charging the wall head-on. At the near end, a wooden watchtower rose above the rest, just a raised platform on four posts with a roof and a bell hanging from the crossbeam.

It wasn't built for war. It was built for warning. A place to ring the bell and grab your spear while whatever was out there was still at the tree line.

Smoke rose from a few hearths, thin and grey against the morning sky.

Eric stopped at the edge of the treeline.

His breath caught.

The village was laid out like a crescent, following the curve of the river. A single dirt path ran through the centre, flanked by houses with low doors and shuttered windows. A well stood in the middle of an open square. Beyond the palisade, a few small fields and vegetable patches. No wheat or barley. Just enough to feed the people who lived here.

It looked exactly like the village he'd been born in.

For a moment, something flickered in his chest. A memory he didn't know he still had. Running barefoot between houses just like these. The smell of smoke and cooking meat. His mother's voice calling him in before dark.

The images came and went like fragments of a dream. No detail. Just feeling. A warmth that had nothing to do with the sun.

Then he looked closer.

The well was on the opposite side. The fields were planted differently. The houses had a slightly different shape to their roofs.

*'Same, but not.'*

He stood there, naked and bleeding, staring at a village that could have been his home if someone had redrawn it from memory with small mistakes.

The cold had been eating at him for hours. The knee had been bleeding the whole time. He hadn't eaten in almost a day. The adrenaline that had carried him through the forest had burned out somewhere along the riverbank, and nothing was left to replace it.

His legs gave out.

He didn't feel himself fall. One moment he was standing, the next he was on his knees, then on his side, the cold ground pressing against his cheek. The village blurred at the edges of his vision. The smoke from the chimneys smeared across the sky like brushstrokes.

He tried to get up. His arms wouldn't move.

*'No. No, I'm almost —'*

Footsteps. Voices. Distant, like they were coming from the end of a long tunnel.

Someone was running toward him.

He tried to lift his head. It felt like it weighed a hundred pounds.

A shape appeared above him. A face. Old. Wrinkled. A beard. The voice said something in a language he hadn't heard since he was six years old. The words were familiar, but they came at him like sounds through deep water.

*'gods... boy... blanket... quick...'*

He caught the meaning, barely. Enough to know it was aimed at someone else and that help was coming.

Eric's eyes stayed open for another few seconds. Then the world went dark.

---

He opened his eyes.

He was indoors. Warm. Something rough and heavy was draped over his body. Wool, he realised. A blanket. He was lying on something soft. Hay, maybe. A bed of hay.

The ceiling was low. Wooden beams. The walls were plastered wattle. A fire crackled somewhere nearby, casting flickering light across the room.

He was indoors, alive, warm beneath the blanket. The fire crackled. The air was still. The world wasn't trying to kill him anymore.

And then, slowly, the panic began to creep back in.

His heart started to race. His breathing quickened. His fingers curled into the blanket, gripping it like it was the only solid thing in the world.

*'Where... where am I?'*

He looked around the room again. Low ceiling. Wooden beams. Firelight. The rough wool scratching his chin.

*'A house. I'm in a house.'*

*'Someone probably found me and carried me inside.'*

He let that sink in for a moment. Let the warmth of the fire and the weight of the blanket settle into his skin.

*'I'm alive.'*

A small feeling pushed through the exhaustion. Gratitude. Quiet and simple. Whoever these people were, they didn't leave him to freeze.

*'Thank you.'*

He didn't know who he was saying it to. The old man. The girl who might come. The strangers who had pulled him out of the cold.

*'I'm not in the forest anymore. I'm okay. For now.'*

Then the thoughts came anyway.

*'What do I tell them? How do I explain showing up naked in the middle of nowhere?'*

*'They're going to ask questions. Questions I can't answer.'*

*'What if they think I'm crazy? What if they throw me out? What if —'*

The door creaked.

He turned his head.

A girl stood in the doorway. Maybe seventeen. Brown hair pulled back. Plain dress, practical apron. A wooden bowl in her hands, steam curling from it.

She saw he was awake.

She smiled.

She said something. The sounds reached his ears and his brain reached for them, clumsy and slow.

*'ah... awake...'*

That was all he caught. Two words out of the whole sentence. The meaning came through anyway, filled in by the softness of her voice and the smile on her face.

She was being kind.

He opened his lips to respond.

Nothing came out.

His throat was locked. His tongue felt like it belonged to someone else. He knew the words somewhere in his memory, buried fourteen years deep, but he couldn't reach them. Couldn't shape them. Couldn't force them past his teeth.

The girl waited.

Eric stared at her, knuckles white on the blanket, and felt the language sit just beyond his reach. Familiar as a forgotten dream, but there. Still there. He just needed to remember how to grab it.
